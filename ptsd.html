What is PTSD?

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) is a mental health condition that can develop after experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event — such as combat, assault, accidents, natural disasters, or sudden loss.

PTSD changes how the brain processes fear and memory, leading to persistent symptoms that disrupt daily life. It is not a sign of weakness — it is a recognized, treatable psychiatric disorder.

Causes & Risk Factors

Not everyone exposed to trauma develops PTSD. Risk is influenced by:

  • Type & Severity: Direct, life-threatening, or repeated trauma increases likelihood
  • Biological: Genetics, stress hormone dysregulation, brain changes
  • Personal: Previous trauma, childhood adversity, difficulty regulating emotions
  • Environmental: Lack of support after trauma, ongoing stress, isolation

Symptoms of PTSD

1. Intrusive Symptoms

  • Recurrent distressing memories or flashbacks
  • Trauma-related nightmares
  • Intense distress at reminders (triggers)

2. Avoidance

  • Avoiding trauma-related places, people, conversations
  • Suppressing thoughts or feelings about the event

3. Negative Changes in Mood & Thinking

  • Persistent negative emotions (fear, guilt, shame)
  • Emotional numbness or detachment
  • Loss of interest in activities
  • Memory gaps about parts of the trauma

4. Hyperarousal & Reactivity

  • Irritability or angry outbursts
  • Hypervigilance, easily startled
  • Difficulty sleeping or concentrating

Treatment Approaches

Psychotherapy

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) – trauma-focused
  • Prolonged Exposure Therapy
  • Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)
  • Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR)

Medication

  • SSRIs (sertraline, paroxetine) – first-line
  • Other antidepressants or adjunctive medications
  • Prazosin for trauma-related nightmares (in some cases)

Lifestyle & Support

  • Safe social connections
  • Mindfulness, yoga, exercise
  • Consistent routines & sleep hygiene
  • Avoiding alcohol/drugs

Conclusion

PTSD is serious, but highly treatable. With trauma-focused therapy (especially Prolonged Exposure, CPT, or EMDR), appropriate medication when needed, and supportive environments, most people can reduce symptoms significantly, process what happened, and rebuild a meaningful life.

Recovery is real — many individuals with PTSD go on to experience renewed stability, connection, and purpose. Seeking help early makes a profound difference.
