What is Depression?

Depression (Major Depressive Disorder) is a serious but treatable medical condition involving persistent sadness, loss of interest, and reduced ability to function. It affects mood, thinking, behavior, and physical health far beyond temporary sadness or grief.

Causes of Depression

Depression typically arises from interacting factors:

  • Biological: Neurotransmitter imbalances (serotonin, dopamine, norepinephrine), genetics, chronic illness, hormonal shifts
  • Psychological: Negative thinking patterns, low self-esteem, unresolved trauma or grief
  • Social/Environmental: Isolation, major life stressors (loss, financial strain), lack of support

Symptoms of Depression

Emotional & Cognitive

  • Persistent sadness, emptiness, or irritability
  • Hopelessness, worthlessness, excessive guilt
  • Loss of interest/pleasure (anhedonia)
  • Difficulty concentrating or deciding
  • Thoughts of death or suicide

Physical & Behavioral

  • Sleep changes (insomnia or hypersomnia)
  • Appetite/weight changes
  • Fatigue or low energy
  • Unexplained aches or digestive issues
  • Social withdrawal, reduced activity

Types of Depression

  • Major Depressive Disorder (MDD): Intense episodes lasting ≥2 weeks
  • Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia): Chronic, milder depression ≥2 years
  • Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD): Seasonal pattern, often winter worsening
  • Postpartum Depression: After childbirth – intense mood, fatigue, anxiety
  • Situational/Adjustment Depression: Triggered by specific stressors or losses

Treatment of Depression

Psychotherapy

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) – restructuring negative thoughts
  • Interpersonal Therapy (IPT) – improving relationships
  • Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT)
  • Supportive & psychodynamic approaches

Medication

  • SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram, fluoxetine) – most common first-line
  • SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine)
  • Atypical antidepressants (bupropion, mirtazapine)
  • Augmentation strategies when needed

Other Treatments

  • Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT) – for severe, treatment-resistant cases
  • Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)
  • Ketamine/esketamine (rapid-acting for severe cases)

Lifestyle

  • Aerobic exercise (30 min most days)
  • Consistent sleep & nutrition
  • Social connection & meaningful activity
  • Stress reduction (mindfulness, yoga)

Conclusion

Depression is common, serious, and highly treatable. With the right combination of therapy (especially CBT), medication when appropriate, lifestyle support, and human connection, most people experience meaningful improvement — often full or near-full recovery.

Reaching out is not weakness; it is strength. Help is effective, recovery is real, and you are not alone.
