1. Definition and Overview

Bipolar disorder is a mood disorder involving periods of unusually elevated mood (mania or hypomania) and periods of depression. These shifts are more severe than normal mood changes and can disrupt sleep, decision-making, relationships, and daily life.

Subtypes include:

  • Bipolar I: At least one full manic episode (often with depression); mania may require hospitalization.
  • Bipolar II: Hypomanic episodes + major depression; no full mania.
  • Cyclothymic Disorder: Milder, chronic hypomanic and depressive symptoms (≥2 years).
  • Other Specified/Unspecified: Bipolar-like symptoms not fitting exact criteria.

2. Causes and Risk Factors

Bipolar disorder arises from a mix of genetic, biological, and environmental influences:

  • Genetic: Strong family history; multiple genes involved.
  • Biological: Neurotransmitter imbalances (serotonin, dopamine, norepinephrine); brain structure/function differences.
  • Environmental: Stress, trauma, substance use, life changes can trigger episodes.
  • Other: Onset often late teens/early adulthood.

3. Symptoms

Manic/Hypomanic Phase

  • Elevated/irritable mood
  • Increased energy/activity
  • Reduced need for sleep
  • Racing thoughts, rapid speech
  • Grandiose ideas, impulsivity (spending, risks)
  • Distraction, poor judgment

Depressive Phase

  • Persistent sadness/hopelessness
  • Fatigue, low energy
  • Loss of interest/pleasure
  • Sleep/appetite changes
  • Concentration issues
  • Worthlessness, guilt, suicidal thoughts

5. Treatment Options

Bipolar is lifelong but highly manageable with:

Medication

  • Mood stabilizers (e.g., lithium)
  • Anticonvulsants (valproate, lamotrigine)
  • Antipsychotics for mania/psychosis
  • Antidepressants (used cautiously)

Psychotherapy

  • CBT: Pattern recognition & coping
  • IPSRT: Routine & sleep stabilization
  • Family-Focused Therapy
  • Psychoeducation

Lifestyle

  • Consistent sleep
  • Exercise & nutrition
  • Stress reduction
  • Avoid substances
  • Support network

Conclusion

Bipolar disorder is manageable with awareness, early diagnosis, and consistent care. Education, support, and treatment adherence enable stable, productive, fulfilling lives. The right combination of medication, therapy, and lifestyle strategies helps individuals regain control and thrive.
